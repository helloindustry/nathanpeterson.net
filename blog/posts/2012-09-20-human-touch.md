---
date: 2012-09-20
title: "Human Touch"
---
<p>I&#8217;m a part of Richwoods Christian Church in Peoria, IL where I live. From time to time I lead worship there. I always find it interesting to evaluate myself as a worship leader from service to service since there are three services per weekend. I think I&#8217;m seeing a pattern&#8230;</p>

<p><strong>The pattern: A+X = B-X</strong></p>

<p><strong>A+X</strong><br />
With each service, technical quality increases. Mistakes decrease. Between the first and the last service we play, there is probably a 10&#8211;25% increase in technical accuracy. From a performance standpoint, things only get better as I learn from my mistakes and apply what I learn to the next time around.</p>

<p>Practice makes perfect. Similar to playing a tour or making a cookie recipe, it should get better over time. Who wouldn&#8217;t want that? But&#8230;</p>

<p><strong>B-X</strong><br />
As technical quality increases, I feel something of value decreasing. I&#8217;m not sure what to call it. Spontaneity maybe. Or realness. The human touch. It seems to decrease with every identical service I play.</p>

<p>I think it&#8217;s because, while I&#8217;ve learned from my mistakes and I don&#8217;t repeat them, I&#8217;ve also learned from my successes and <em>I try my very best</em> to repeat them. This is very dangerous:</p>

<ul>
<li>It takes away from the humanity of the next crowd. I assume the people I&#8217;m about to play for are the same as the ones I just played for, and in doing so I essentially ignore them as individuals.</li>
<li>It takes away from my humanity, offered to them. I can&#8217;t count how many times a mistake of mine was the most powerful point of the service for someone. It&#8217;s because they realized we&#8217;re the same - that they&#8217;re not alone.</li>
<li>It takes away from my dependance on God to <em>speak</em> in order for me to know how to act. So I stop listening for His voice.</li>
</ul>

<p>So that&#8217;s the pattern. I think most of us are susceptible to it. And I think it should concern us.</p>

<p>If we&#8217;re not careful, by pursing perfection and mistake-reduction, we can withhold the very thing people need most from us. <strong>We trade authentic healing which can only come from human touch for the fleeting high of impressing and being impressed.</strong></p>

<p>I&#8217;m not exactly sure how to get better at this. I&#8217;m pretty sure it doesn&#8217;t look like purposefully sabotaging myself (put a dead battery in my mic, hold in pee to the point of explosion right before heading on stage, etc). But it probably comes down to <strong>developing the discipline of recognizing the individuality of each person I play for and listening for God&#8217;s voice, every moment of every time I&#8217;m on stage</strong>. This discipline is probably valuable in any situation where one human is trying to give something of value to another human. </p>

<p>I&#8217;m all for increasing technical quality. But not at the expense of humanity. <strong>People do not come to church to be impressed.</strong> (Though it&#8217;s often times how we operate.) They come to find other humans, just like them. Flawed. Scared. Lonely. Confused. Imperfect.</p>

<p>No matter how efficient our methods become, there is no substitute for human touch.</p>
